Clear Language

Throughout most of my life I have been fascinated with language. The role of language in our lives is inescapable, as the content of our minds is largely derived from our language. Because of this, an awareness of one's language (both inner and outer) can give deep insight into the structure of one's mind and beliefs. In particular, much of our language is chosen "unconsciously", and because of that we are often unaware of it. One of the things which I have wondered recently:

Why do people declare things to be their opposites?

This is a tricky idea to wrangle into words. When you make a statement, you declare something to be a certain way. "The window is clean" declares that the window-in-question is clean. The statement is straightforward and requires little-to-no consideration to understand. What if I were to tell you, "the window is not clean"?

Effectively, this is a pretty useless statement, because it lacks substance. The statement tells us nothing about what the window is. It could even be a more specific description of "clean" (Meaning, that someone could say something like, "It's not clean; it's almost clean." People love to be specific.), so we are unable to rule out "clean" as a possibility entirely. With such a contrived example, it is easy to write this off as a trivial novelty of language, though these expressions litter our language like little clouds of confusion.

Here are some more examples.

"He wasn't there." ⇛ Well, where was he?

"That's not what I want." ⇛ What do you want, then?

"I don't think so." ⇛ What do you think, then?

"I'm not hungry." ⇛ Then what are you??

"I haven't seen it yet." ⇛ No? You'll get there.

If you want to make a negative statement like that and still get any specific message communicated, you must make another declaration. In other words, unclear language forces the recipient to make assumptions or guesses, which inevitably breed misunderstanding. How might we improve this? Well, the general motion is to move from stating unreality to stating reality. This can be approached by using specific, descriptive words instead of negating the verb "to be", such as saying something is "invisible" instead of that it "can't be seen". Later it could be expanded upon by recognizing when one is stating something without substance, and choosing to state what is needed by the conversation and moment. The most important factor in this is one's continual attention towards oneself. These are habits that tend to dissolve only over extended time.
